## Overview

This skill helps founders choose between the two primary US corporate entities: Limited Liability Companies (LLCs) and C Corporations. Each has advantages depending on your business goals, investment plans, and tax situation.

---

## Quick Decision Framework

### Choose C Corporation If:
- ✅ Planning to raise VC/angel investment
- ✅ Want to give employees stock options
- ✅ Building to scale large (potential IPO/acquisition)
- ✅ Multiple international owners
- ✅ Want standardized, well-understood structure

### Choose LLC If:
- ✅ Solo founder or small partnership
- ✅ Bootstrapping / not planning to raise money
- ✅ Want simpler setup and administration
- ✅ Pass-through taxation benefits you
- ✅ Side project or freelance work
- ✅ Don't know what you want to be yet

## What is an LLC?

**Limited Liability Company** - organized under an operating agreement (contract between owners/members)

### Key Characteristics

- **Flexible**: Almost endless possibilities for structure
- **Pass-through taxation**: No entity-level tax; income flows to owners' personal returns
- **Limited liability**: Owners' personal assets protected from business debts
- **Less standardized**: Must examine operating agreement to understand governance

### Advantages

1. **Lower incorporation costs**
2. **Easier ongoing administration**
3. **Pass-through taxation** can be more efficient for smaller firms
4. **Money and IP flow freely** between members and LLC without tax consequences
5. **Less ceremony** for major actions (no board resolutions needed)

### Disadvantages

1. **Investors don't like them** - requires substantial due diligence
2. **Complicated for international owners** - may trigger US tax obligations
3. **Employee equity is messy** - no clean stock option mechanism
4. **No standardized vesting** - departures require negotiation
5. **May need to convert later** - costly and time-consuming

---

## What is a C Corporation?

**C Corporation** - designed to separate operators from owners, with ownership tracked by shares

### Key Characteristics

- **Shareholders own** the company (can be uninvolved in operations)
- **Board of Directors governs** major decisions
- **Officers handle** day-to-day operations
- **Corporate taxation**: Company pays taxes on profits; shareholders taxed separately on dividends/salary
- **Highly standardized**: Delaware has centuries of established law

### Advantages

1. **Investors love them** - standard terms, familiar structure
2. **Clean employee equity** - well-understood stock options
3. **Works internationally** - no complex tax situations for foreign owners
4. **Established vesting norms** - standard 4-year, 1-year cliff
5. **Delaware Court of Chancery** - predictable, pro-management legal outcomes
6. **IPO-ready**: 90%+ of US IPOs are Delaware C Corps

### Disadvantages

1. **Double taxation**: Corporate profits taxed, then dividends taxed again
2. **More expensive** to set up and maintain
3. **More ceremony** required (board resolutions, formal votes)
4. **More rigid** structure

## Detailed Comparison

### Assets and IP

| LLC | C Corporation |
|-----|---------------|
| IP flows freely between members and LLC | IP transfers have tax consequences |
| Minimal ceremony for transfers | Requires formal documentation |
| Great for side projects | Better for scaling businesses |

**LLC Advantage**: If you're not sure what your project will become, LLC lets you easily move things around.

### Taxation

| LLC | C Corporation |
|-----|---------------|
| Pass-through to owners | Entity-level taxation |
| Losses offset owner's other income | Losses carry forward |
| No double taxation | Profits taxed twice |

**LLC Tax Example**: Company loses $10,000 in year one. LLC owner can reduce personal income by $10,000 and potentially get substantial refund. C Corp carries loss forward to future profitable years.

**C Corp Tax Example**: Tech worker with $150k salary founds LLC that loses $10k. Personal taxable income becomes $140k, potentially saving thousands in taxes immediately.

### International Owners

| LLC | C Corporation |
|-----|---------------|
| Creates complex tax situations | Clean international structure |
| Non-US owners may owe US taxes | Standard corporate treatment |
| May have dual taxation in home country | Well-understood by tax treaties |

**Example Problem**: LLC with US owner + Japan owner. Japan owner:
- Taxed by US on LLC income
- May also be taxed by Japan on same income
- Complex reporting requirements

**C Corp Solution**: Japan owner only taxed when receiving salary or dividends - standard, predictable.

### Investment

| LLC | C Corporation |
|-----|---------------|
| Investors must do extensive due diligence | Standardized investment terms |
| May require conversion before investment | Ready for investment as-is |
| Y Combinator requires conversion | YC and most VCs prefer Delaware C Corp |

> "The extreme flexibility available in the LLC form also means that investors attempting to invest in one will have to do substantial expensive legal work to ensure that they are buying what they expect to be buying." - Orrick Legal Guide

### Employee Equity

| LLC | C Corporation |
|-----|---------------|
| Giving ownership is non-trivial | Stock options are well-understood |
| May complicate employee's taxes | Predictable tax treatment |
| No standard mechanisms | ISOs, NSOs, RSUs all established |
| Employees uncomfortable becoming members | Employees expect stock options |

### Vesting

| LLC | C Corporation |
|-----|---------------|
| No standard vesting | Standard 4-year vesting common |
| Departure requires negotiation | Clean departure mechanics |
| Operating agreement governs | Established legal frameworks |

## Formation Location

Both LLCs and C Corps are formed at state level, not federal.

### Why Delaware?

- **60%+ of Fortune 500** incorporated there
- **Separate business court** (Court of Chancery)
- **Centuries of case law** - high predictability
- **Pro-management decisions** - VCs feel comfortable
- **Simple conversion** from LLC to C Corp

**Note**: You'll likely need to register as "foreign" entity in your operating state (straightforward process, modest fee).

## Decision Tree

```
Are you planning to raise VC/angel investment?
├── YES → C Corporation
└── NO → Continue...

Will you have international co-founders or owners?
├── YES → C Corporation (simpler tax treatment)
└── NO → Continue...

Do you want to offer employees stock options?
├── YES → C Corporation
└── NO → Continue...

Is this a side project, consulting, or freelance work?
├── YES → LLC (simpler, cheaper)
└── NO → Continue...

Are you bootstrapping with no exit plans?
├── YES → LLC (pass-through taxation helps)
└── NO → Continue...

Not sure yet?
└── LLC (can convert later, easier to start)
```

## How to Help Founders

### Discovery Questions

1. **"What are your funding plans?"**
   - Raising money → C Corp
   - Bootstrapping → Either works, LLC simpler

2. **"Where are you and your co-founders located?"**
   - All US → Either works
   - International owners → C Corp strongly preferred

3. **"Will you hire employees and give them equity?"**
   - Yes → C Corp
   - No/Maybe → Either works

4. **"Is this a side project or full-time venture?"**
   - Side project → LLC
   - Full-time with growth plans → C Corp

5. **"Do you have existing IP to contribute?"**
   - Yes, want it simple → LLC (easier IP flow)
   - Yes, want it documented → C Corp (cleaner records)

### Common Misconceptions to Address

1. **"LLCs aren't real businesses"**
   - False. Basecamp is an LLC. Facebook started as one.
   - US treats both as legitimate corporate entities.

2. **"I can just convert later"**
   - True, but conversion is costly and time-consuming.
   - If you know you're raising money, start as C Corp.

3. **"C Corps have double taxation so they're always worse"**
   - Not necessarily. C Corp losses carry forward.
   - LLC pass-through can create complex situations.
   - Tax efficiency depends on your specific situation.

4. **"I need a C Corp to be taken seriously"**
   - Only matters if raising institutional investment.
   - For customers and partners, both are legitimate.

### Red Flags

- Choosing LLC but mentioning VC aspirations
- International co-founders choosing LLC
- Planning employee equity but choosing LLC
- Choosing C Corp for solo consulting business

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **C Corp for investment track** - VCs expect it, don't fight this
2. **LLC for simplicity** - lower cost, less ceremony, pass-through taxes
3. **International owners → C Corp** - avoid tax complexity
4. **Employee equity → C Corp** - established mechanisms
5. **Conversion is possible but costly** - choose wisely upfront
6. **Delaware for either** - best legal framework in US
