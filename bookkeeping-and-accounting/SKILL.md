## Overview

This skill helps founders understand and implement proper bookkeeping and accounting practices for their startups. If you're getting paid, you need to track money moving in and out of your business.

---

## Core Concepts

### What is Bookkeeping vs Accounting?

**Bookkeeping**: Recording details about transactions to the books (ledgers) of the company
- Historically done by detail-oriented specialists
- Now largely computerized

**Accounting**: Turns bookkeeping data into conclusions about business health
- Advises on business structure
- Designs procedures for novel transactions
- Helps with tax planning and returns
- Creates balance sheets and financial reports

### What Are "Books"?

Books are records describing the movement of value into and out of the business.

Each ledger entry has:
- Amount
- Description
- Date
- Where money is coming from
- Where money is going

**Double-Entry Bookkeeping**: Every transaction recorded twice - as a credit to one account and a debit to another. This revolutionary 1400s technology makes errors and fraud less likely.

## Critical Principle: Separate Business and Personal Finances

### Why This Matters

1. **Piercing the Corporate Veil**: If you treat the company as a personal pocket, creditors can void your limited liability protection
2. **Operational Simplicity**: Easier bookkeeping when transactions are clearly categorized
3. **Financial Clarity**: Helps surface cash flow issues before they become unsolvable

### How to Implement

**Revenue**:
- Open a business checking account as primary holding place
- Route ALL revenue through this account (Stripe payouts, checks, etc.)
- Deposits will almost all be revenue items, easy to match against sales

**Non-Revenue Deposits** (investment, loans to company):
- Keep these infrequent
- Document appropriately
- Monthly injections OK; multiple times per week is NOT

**Expenses**:
- Get 1-3 credit cards dedicated to business expenses
- NEVER put personal expenses on business cards
- NEVER make mixed purchases (business + personal items) - split into separate transactions
- Pay cards from business checking account ONLY
- Minimize cash transactions
- Keep records of all reimbursements

**Loans FROM Business to Entrepreneur**: 
⚠️ **AVOID THESE**
- Investors hate them
- Complicate bookkeeping
- Weaken your corporate veil argument
- Can be incorrectly recorded as revenue, increasing tax burden
- IRS has strict "related-party loan" documentation rules
- If you need credit, get it from a bank instead

## Accounting Methods

### Cash Accounting
- Record revenue when "available to you without restriction"
- If customer thinks they paid, you've booked revenue
- Expenses booked when actually paid
- Simple and easy to understand
- Common for early-stage businesses

### Accrual Accounting
- Revenue and expenses recognized when:
  - Amount is fixed
  - Known to be collectible
  - Economic performance has happened
- Example: Accepting payment but not shipping yet = no revenue until shipping
- More accurately reflects business health
- **Required by most investors** (can reveal manipulation in cash method)
- If you're on investment track, start with accrual to avoid re-doing books later

## Substantiation Requirements

### What the IRS Requires
- Documentation for ANY transaction on your books
- Most important for larger transactions
- Critical for travel, entertainment, gifts

### Record Keeping Best Practices

**Revenue Items**:
- Keep copies of receipts and invoices
- For large transactions, maintain contracts or documentation
- Organize by year minimum
- After system migrations, save old receipts somewhere permanent

**Expense Items**:
- Policy: Every purchase requires written receipt/invoice
- Centralize receipts (e.g., receipts@company.com email)
- Treat receiptless transactions as anomalies
- Create written records immediately for undocumented transactions
- Keep ALL bank statements and credit card statements indefinitely (banks may delete them)

**Travel, Transportation, Entertainment, Gifts** (IRS scrutinizes these):
- Keep contemporaneous written records
- Spreadsheet for each employee showing:
  - Travel dates
  - Where they stayed (with receipts)
  - Business purpose
- For meals: record participants and business purpose on receipt
- See IRS Publication 463 for details

### Retention Period
- General rule: 3 years after filing tax return for that year
- Practical advice: Store indefinitely (disk space is cheap)

## Checklist: Setting Up Proper Bookkeeping

### Immediate Actions
- [ ] Open dedicated business checking account
- [ ] Get 1-2 dedicated business credit cards
- [ ] Set up receipts@company.com or receipt tracking software
- [ ] Choose accounting method (cash vs accrual)
- [ ] Select bookkeeping software

### Ongoing Practices
- [ ] Route ALL revenue to business account
- [ ] Keep business and personal expenses completely separate
- [ ] Never make mixed purchases
- [ ] Document all transactions immediately
- [ ] Keep ALL financial statements indefinitely
- [ ] For travel/entertainment: record participants + business purpose

### When to Get Professional Help
- [ ] Hire bookkeeper when you have 100+ transactions/month
- [ ] Get accountant involved for tax planning and returns
- [ ] Consult accountant before any business structure changes

## How to Help Founders

### Common Questions to Address

1. **"Do I need bookkeeping software?"**
   - Yes, even a simple one. Excel works initially but grows painful.
   - Popular options: QuickBooks, Xero, Wave (free)

2. **"Should I use cash or accrual accounting?"**
   - Early stage with no investor plans? Cash is simpler.
   - Planning to raise money? Start with accrual.
   - Switching later is painful and expensive.

3. **"How do I pay myself?"**
   - Regular salary or quarterly distributions
   - Avoid frequent ad-hoc transfers
   - Document everything

4. **"I mixed personal and business expenses..."**
   - Clean it up now. Create clear records.
   - Going forward, never do this again.
   - Consider this a serious warning.

5. **"What records do I need to keep?"**
   - Everything. Every receipt, invoice, bank statement.
   - Organized by year.
   - Indefinitely.

### Red Flags to Watch For
- Multiple weekly transfers between personal and business
- Missing receipts for transactions
- Loans between owner and company
- Mixed personal/business credit card usage
- No centralized receipt storage

### When to Escalate to Accountant
- Setting up multi-entity structures
- International operations
- Investment discussions
- Tax planning decisions
- Any IRS correspondence

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Separate everything**: Business money and personal money should never mix
2. **Document everything**: If it's not written down, it didn't happen (for tax purposes)
3. **Choose your method wisely**: Cash for simplicity, accrual for investment track
4. **Store indefinitely**: Digital storage is cheaper than recreating records
5. **Get professional help early**: Accountants save money and stress
