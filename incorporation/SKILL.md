## Overview

This skill helps founders decide whether and when to incorporate their business. A business exists when you say it does. A company is a specific registered structure. Incorporation is the legal process of turning one into the other.

---

## Business vs Company

### Business (Unincorporated)
- Exists as soon as you start providing goods/services for money
- No separate legal existence from owner
- **Sole proprietorship**: One owner
- **Partnership**: Multiple owners
- ~27 million in the US (vs ~6 million incorporated)

### Company (Incorporated)
- Registered structure with rights and responsibilities
- Separate legal entity from owners
- Limited liability protection
- Can own property, sign contracts, sue and be sued

---

## Benefits of Incorporation

> "The primary reason for selecting a corporate form is for the limited liability and perpetual existence that these organisations can provide, because once a company is formed, it is regarded as a separate legal entity from its owners." - Orrick Legal Guide

### 1. Limited Liability Protection
- Sole proprietors are **personally liable** for all business debts
- Company owners are generally **not personally liable**
- Your house, savings, personal assets are protected
- Business obligations stay with the business

### 2. Ownership Clarity
- Clear ownership interests for founders, investors, employees
- Everyone knows what they're getting
- Centuries of established law govern equity

### 3. Business as Property
- Company can be owned, bought, sold
- Can be borrowed against
- Can be inherited or transferred
- Exists separate from founders

### 4. Professional Signal
- Shows customers and partners you're serious
- Indicates intent to operate professionally
- Required by many enterprise customers

## When to Incorporate

### Incorporate IMMEDIATELY If:

**1. Professional advisors tell you to**
- Some businesses have inherent liability exposure
- Your lawyer/accountant can assess your specific situation

**2. You have substantial personal assets**
- House, savings, other business interests
- Need protection from business debts/liabilities

**3. You have partners**
- Unincorporated partnerships have significant drawbacks
- Clear mechanisms for adding/removing partners
- Established frameworks for handling departures

**4. You want to share ownership**
- With employees or advisors
- Equity has predictable, enforceable rights
- Better than vague informal agreements

### Incorporate BEFORE:

**5. Taking investment**
- Investors want clear ownership documentation
- Most require corporate entity
- Often require Delaware C Corporation specifically

**6. Hiring first full-time employee**
- Employment is heavily regulated
- You may be liable for employee's actions
- Quantum leap in business complexity

### Incorporate AS:

**7. Business approaches material size**
- More products, more customers = more risk
- Sophisticated customers more likely to sue
- Attracts attention of bad actors
- Guideline: Many businesses incorporate above ~$100K revenue

## Why NOT to Incorporate

The main reason: **Complexity and cost**

- Sole proprietorship exists when you say it does
- Company is like a puppy: expensive ongoing upkeep
- Even when you're tired of it, still need to feed it
- Filing obligations, fees, compliance requirements

## Types of Companies (US)

### C Corporation

**Best for**: Investment-track startups, employee equity, large scale

**Stats**: 
- 90%+ of US IPOs (2007-2014) were Delaware C Corps
- Overwhelming choice of tech companies and investors

**Why Delaware?**
- 60%+ of Fortune 500 incorporated there
- Separate business court (Court of Chancery)
- Long-established body of law
- High predictability in legal disputes
- Pro-management decisions
- VCs feel comfortable and familiar

### LLC (Limited Liability Company)

**Best for**: Solo entrepreneurs, consultants, freelancers, bootstrapped businesses

**Advantages**:
- Lower incorporation cost
- Easier ongoing administration
- Pass-through taxation
- Limited liability protection

**Common pattern**: Start as LLC, convert to C Corp if/when raising money

### S Corporation

Not a separate company type - it's a **tax election**:
- Corporate form of C Corp
- Pass-through taxation of LLC
- Specific requirements and limitations
- IRS has details at irs.gov

## Who Can Incorporate?

### No Restrictions on Ownership

> "There are no US federal or state laws that require a stockholder or LLC member to be a US citizen or permanent resident to form a US company." - Orrick Legal Guide

**Anyone can**:
- Own all shares of a US corporation
- Be sole member of US LLC
- Be board member or officer (no shares required)
- Be non-US national and non-resident

**This is normal**:
- Millions of US corps controlled by people outside US
- Foreigners routinely transact business in US
- US entities used for everything from flats to factories

## LLC vs C Corp Decision

| Factor | Choose LLC | Choose C Corp |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Funding plans | Bootstrapping | Raising investment |
| Co-founders | All US-based | International |
| Employee equity | Not planned | Want stock options |
| Stage | Side project | Full-time venture |
| Complexity tolerance | Want simple | Can handle more |
| Growth expectations | Modest | Aiming big |

See the detailed [LLC vs C Corp guide](llc-vs-c-corp) for more.

## Common Myths

### "I need to be a US citizen"
❌ FALSE - Anyone can incorporate a US company

### "LLCs aren't 'real' businesses"
❌ FALSE - Both LLCs and C Corps are legitimate, well-understood

### "I should incorporate in my home state"
❌ USUALLY FALSE - Delaware is preferred for most tech companies regardless of location

### "Incorporating is expensive"
❌ PARTIALLY FALSE - Initial incorporation is reasonable; ongoing compliance has costs

### "I can incorporate later when I need to"
⚠️ RISKY - Some things are easier to do upfront (IP assignment, founder vesting)

## Checklist: Should I Incorporate Now?

### Incorporate Now If ANY of These Are True:
- [ ] Professional advisor recommends it
- [ ] You have partners
- [ ] You're taking investment
- [ ] You're hiring employees
- [ ] You want to share equity
- [ ] You have personal assets to protect
- [ ] Business has material liability exposure
- [ ] Revenue approaching $100K

### Maybe Wait If ALL of These Are True:
- [ ] Solo founder
- [ ] No investment plans
- [ ] No employees
- [ ] Low liability exposure
- [ ] Early experimentation phase
- [ ] Minimal revenue

## How to Help Founders

### Discovery Questions

1. **"What's your business model?"**
   - High liability (services, advice, physical products) → Incorporate
   - Low liability (digital products, solo consulting) → Maybe wait

2. **"Do you have partners?"**
   - Yes → Incorporate (formalize relationship)
   - No → Continue evaluation

3. **"Are you planning to raise money?"**
   - Yes → Incorporate as C Corp
   - No → LLC or wait

4. **"Will you hire employees?"**
   - Yes → Incorporate before first hire
   - No → Continue evaluation

5. **"What's your revenue?"**
   - Approaching/above $100K → Strongly consider incorporating
   - Early stage → Lower urgency

6. **"Do you have personal assets to protect?"**
   - Yes (house, savings, other businesses) → Incorporate
   - No → Lower urgency

### Common Founder Situations

**"I have a side project making $500/month"**
→ Probably fine as sole proprietor for now. Consider LLC when revenue grows or if you want liability protection.

**"I'm starting a consulting business"**
→ LLC makes sense. Simple, pass-through taxation, liability protection.

**"My co-founder and I are building an app"**
→ Incorporate. You need clear ownership structure.

**"We're applying to Y Combinator"**
→ Delaware C Corporation, no question.

**"I'm in Germany but want US customers"**
→ US incorporation can help. C Corp if planning US investment.

### Red Flags

- Partners without formal agreement
- Planning investment but not incorporated
- Hiring employees as sole proprietor
- High-liability business without incorporation
- Vague ownership promises to co-founders

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Incorporation = separate legal entity** with liability protection
2. **Most businesses don't incorporate** but most growing businesses do
3. **Partners should incorporate** to formalize relationship
4. **Investment requires incorporation** (usually Delaware C Corp)
5. **Incorporate before hiring** employees
6. **Anyone can incorporate** in US regardless of citizenship
7. **Delaware is preferred** for most tech companies
